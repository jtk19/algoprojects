<div class='post hentry' itemprop='blogPost' itemscope='itemscope' itemtype='http://schema.org/BlogPosting'>

<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
How I Get Over The Writer's Block
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-3406366176371917906' itemprop='description articleBody'>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-pWZQXdxgR6M/UknNTWi4gII/AAAAAAAABMs/MZB99I2x8SA/s1600/writers-block.jpg" imageanchor="1"><img alt="Jac Wright, Author, The Reckless Engineer" border="0" height="240" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-pWZQXdxgR6M/UknNTWi4gII/AAAAAAAABMs/MZB99I2x8SA/s320/writers-block.jpg" title="Jac Wright" width="320" /></a></div>
<br />
I write in spells. &nbsp;At times I might write a dozen hours a day for over a week, and then I might not write anything again for several weeks. &nbsp;Sometimes I might write for a few hours a day for weeks. &nbsp;I find I cannot force myself to write when I feel the block. If I do, the writing comes out contrived and not feeling right. Inspiration has to come to me naturally. &nbsp;I just have to leave the manuscript aside and do something else until the words and ideas start to flow again.<br />
<br />
I hit the dreaded block several times during the course of writing <b><i><a href="http://therecklessengineerbook.blogspot.co.uk/" target="_blank">The Reckless Engineer</a>.</i></b><br />
<br />
I do a couple of things when I hit the Writer's Block. &nbsp;The first thing I try is setting the manuscript aside and reading a good book. &nbsp;I hit a very difficult spot just before the scene in Chapter 15 of <b><i><a href="http://therecklessengineerbook.blogspot.co.uk/" target="_blank">The Reckless Engineer</a></i></b>. &nbsp;I felt that the part of chapter 15 I had written was dull and was slowing down the pace of the plot. Try as I might I could not think of a way to maneuver&nbsp;the plot to pick up the pace again. &nbsp;At this point I decided to put the writing aside and read a book. &nbsp;I read two Agatha Christie books, <i>At Bertram&#8217;s Hotel</i> and <i>Cards on the Table</i>, at this point. &nbsp;Then, when I returned to my writing about a week later, I decided it was time to bring the character Jack Connor home. &nbsp;I had kept him in custody until that point. &nbsp;I then deleted the part of that chapter I had previously written and started writing in this new direction. &nbsp;Everything came easily to me after that insight into how I should progress the plot which had to occur to me in a moment of inspiration in its own time.<br />
<br />
I reached a second nasty block when I needed to write the scenes with Jeremy at a Portsmouth seaside hotel, The Royal Atlantic. &nbsp;This time I knew the plot, but the prose was not coming out right. &nbsp;I had moved out of Portsmouth by then, but I decided to take three days off and check into The Royal Beach Hotel in Southsea, Portsmouth to see if I could get the words flowing again. &nbsp;I did the same again, volunteering at a back-stage to help a friend at the Gielgud theatre, to write the scene set in the London West End.<br />
<br />
I do not write while I am at the scene. &nbsp;I just immerse myself in the environment and absorb the people, the sense of the surroundings, the sounds, and the views. I interact with the people and I might take some photographs. &nbsp;I come out of the scene and do something entirely different for about a week, letting the ideas and the images work their magic at the back of my mind. Then when I sit down to write again the words just flow naturally.<br />
<br />
These two techniques &#8211; reading a good book or two and immersing myself in the scenery I want to write about &#8211; have always helped me out of brief spells of the Writer's Block. &nbsp;They have never failed to get me writing again.<br />
<br />
